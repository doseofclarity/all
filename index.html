Acute Lymphocytic Leukemia in Children

Epidemiology · Diagnosis · Treatment · Nursing Care | Adele Pillitteri, 7e

Definition & Epidemiology

Acute Lymphocytic Leukemia (ALL) – a malignant clonal disease characterized by uncontrolled proliferation of immature lymphoid cells (lymphoblasts) in the bone marrow, blood, and extramedullary sites. It is the most common pediatric cancer, accounting for ~75% of leukemias in children under 15 years[reference:0].

📊 Global & Philippines Incidence
ALL incidence ~3.3 per 100,000 children[reference:1]. In the Philippines, acute leukemia (ALL + AML) accounts for 47.8% of pediatric malignancies, with an incidence of 109 per 100,000[reference:2].
📌 Age & Sex Distribution
Peak incidence 2–5 years; male > female (slightly). 60% of ALL occurs in children; second peak after age 50[reference:3]. In the Philippines, 64.6% of ALL patients are male, mean age 6.4±4.4 years[reference:4].
⚠️ Key point: Approximately 3,500 new leukemia cases are diagnosed annually in the Philippines, equivalent to 10 children diagnosed with cancer per day[reference:5].

Etiology & Risk Factors

⚠️ Genetic & Hereditary Factors
• Down syndrome (highest risk)
• Li‑Fraumeni, neurofibromatosis, Fanconi anemia, ataxia telangiectasia
• Somatic polymorphisms: ARID5B, IKZF1, CDKN2A[reference:6]
• Germline PAX5, ETV6, TP53 mutations[reference:7]
🌍 Environmental & Other Factors
• Ionizing radiation exposure
• Benzene, pesticides
• Previous chemotherapy/radiotherapy
• Epstein‑Barr virus, HIV
• Male sex, Hispanic ethnicity[reference:8]
• Age (peak 2‑5 years)
📌 Most cases are sporadic (acquired mutations), not inherited. The exact cause remains unknown in most children[reference:9].

Pathophysiology & Classification

  • Pathophysiology: Acquired genetic mutations in lymphoid progenitor cells → uncontrolled proliferation of lymphoblasts (>20% in bone marrow) → crowding out normal hematopoiesis → anemia, neutropenia, thrombocytopenia. Extramedullary infiltration (CNS, testes, lymph nodes, liver, spleen) is common[reference:10].
  • Immunophenotypic subtypes (Philippines data): B‑ALL 90%, T‑ALL 10%[reference:11]. In B‑ALL, CD19 (96%), CD10 (96%), cCD79a (97%), cTdT (96%) expression is typical[reference:12].
  • Genetic subgroups (WHO 5th edition): Hyperdiploidy (favorable), t(12;21) ETV6‑RUNX1 (favorable), t(9;22) BCR‑ABL1 (Philadelphia chromosome, poor prognosis), KMT2A rearrangements (infant ALL, poor), iAMP21, hypodiploidy[reference:13].
Philadelphia chromosome (t(9;22)) is present in ~3‑5% of pediatric ALL, historically poor prognosis, now treatable with tyrosine kinase inhibitors.

Clinical Presentation & Diagnosis

🩸 Signs & Symptoms (due to cytopenias)
• Fatigue, pallor (anemia)
• Fever, recurrent infections (neutropenia)
• Easy bruising, petechiae, bleeding (thrombocytopenia)
• Bone/joint pain
• Lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly
• CNS signs: headache, vomiting, papilledema (if CNS involvement)
🔬 Diagnostic Workup
• CBC with differential: anemia, thrombocytopenia, WBC may be low, normal, or high. Blasts on peripheral smear (57% of cases)[reference:14].
• Bone marrow aspiration & biopsy: >20% lymphoblasts.
• Immunophenotyping (flow cytometry): confirms lineage (B‑ALL vs T‑ALL).
• Cytogenetics & molecular: karyotype, FISH, RT‑PCR for fusion genes.[reference:15]
• Lumbar puncture: assess CNS involvement.
• Chemistry panel: LDH, uric acid (tumor lysis).
Key laboratory clue: Elevated LDH, hyperuricemia, and cytopenias with blasts.

Treatment (Chemotherapy, CNS Prophylaxis, Transplant)

💊 Chemotherapy (3 phases)
• Induction (4‑6 weeks): achieve remission (target <5% blasts). Drugs: vincristine, glucocorticoids, anthracycline, asparaginase.
• Consolidation (intensification): eliminate residual disease. High‑dose methotrexate, cytarabine, etc.
• Maintenance: daily oral mercaptopurine, weekly methotrexate for 2‑3 years to prevent relapse[reference:16].
🧠 CNS Prophylaxis
• Intrathecal chemotherapy (methotrexate, cytarabine, hydrocortisone) to prevent CNS relapse.
• Cranial irradiation (reserved for high‑risk or overt CNS disease). Improves CNS control[reference:17].
🩸 Targeted therapy & immunotherapy
• Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (imatinib, dasatinib) for Ph+ ALL.
• Blinatumomab (bispecific T‑cell engager), inotuzumab ozogamicin, CAR T‑cell therapy (tisagenlecleucel) for relapsed/refractory ALL.
💉 Hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT)
• Allogeneic transplant considered for high‑risk or relapsed patients (e.g., Ph+ ALL, hypodiploidy, poor early response).
Supportive care: infection prevention (antibiotics, antifungals), transfusions, hydration/rasburicase for tumor lysis, growth factors.

Nursing Care & Complications

  • Infection prevention: Hand hygiene, reverse isolation during neutropenia, monitor for fever, administer prophylactic antibiotics/antifungals, educate on signs of sepsis.
  • Bleeding precautions: Soft toothbrush, avoid IM injections, use pressure after venipuncture, monitor platelets (transfuse if <10,000 or bleeding).
  • Nutritional support: Manage nausea/vomiting (antiemetics), mucositis (oral care), maintain hydration, consider TPN if severe.
  • Tumor lysis syndrome (TLS) monitoring: Monitor uric acid, potassium, phosphorus, calcium, urine output; administer allopurinol/rasburicase, IV fluids.
  • Psychosocial support: Age‑appropriate education, play therapy, school re‑entry, support groups for parents, palliative care when needed.
  • Long‑term follow‑up: Monitor for late effects (cardiotoxicity, secondary cancers, neurocognitive deficits, endocrinopathies).[reference:18]
Mnemonic “C.A.R.E.” – Chemotherapy side effects, Assess for bleeding/infection, Reduce tumor lysis risk, Educate family.

Prognosis & Long‑Term Follow‑Up

  • 5‑year overall survival (childhood ALL): ~90‑94% in high‑income countries[reference:19][reference:20]. Survival has improved from 10% in the 1970s to >86% overall[reference:21].
  • Prognostic factors: • Favorable: age 1‑9 years, WBC <50,000, hyperdiploidy, t(12;21)
    • Unfavorable: infant ALL (<1 year), WBC >50,000, hypodiploidy, Ph+ ALL, KMT2A rearrangement, poor early response to induction.
  • Relapse risk: ~15‑20% of children relapse; CNS relapse <5% with modern prophylaxis[reference:22]. Relapse treatment includes chemotherapy, immunotherapy, HSCT.
  • Late effects of treatment: Neurocognitive deficits, cardiomyopathy (anthracyclines), endocrine dysfunction (growth hormone deficiency, hypothyroidism, infertility), secondary malignancies (especially CNS irradiation)[reference:23].
  • Long‑term monitoring: Survivorship clinic – cardiac evaluation, endocrine assessment, neuropsychological testing, education on healthy lifestyle, prevention of second cancers.
🌟 Hope for families: With modern risk‑adapted therapy, most children with ALL become long‑term survivors and lead productive lives.
